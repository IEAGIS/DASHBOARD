How are we doing?

What is Gross Domestic Product (GDP)?

GDP provides an overall indicator of an economy’s output and measures the market value of goods, services, and structures produced by that economy over a particular period.

Why is this important?

GDP is the predominate measure of economic activity and includes household consumption, businesses investment in facilities and equipment, purchases by various governmental units, and trading activities with other economies. Tracking GDP and it components overtime provides information about what is happening in the economy.

How do we compare?

How do economies within Arkansas interact and how does the state’s overall economy fit into the region and the nation?

GDP by Region and State

The Texas economy is larger than the economies of all other surrounding states combined. The remaining five states have more similarly sized economies. GDP by State in Region

Texas accounts for 55.7% of the region’s GDP. At 11.4% and 10.4%, Tennessee and Missouri occupy the next largest share of the region’s economic activity. Louisiana and Oklahoma hold the middle position at 8.2% and 6.3%, with Mississippi and Arkansas comprising the smallest share of the region’s GDP at 3.8% and 4.2 %. GDP by Metropolitan Statistical Area (MSA)

In looking at the economic activities during 2001-2015 for the metropolitan areas of Arkansas, the Little Rock-North Little Rock-Conway (LR-NLR-C) had the largest economy, followed by the Fayetteville Springdale Rodgers-metro (FSR) and Fort Smith. Comparatively, Jonesboro, Hot Springs, and Pine Buff are small economies with GDPs under $5 billion. The metropolitan areas of LR-NLR-C and FSR made up 75% of the state’s metro economy in 2015. When combined with Jonesboro and Fort Smith, this figure increases to 93%. Hot Springs and Pine Buff are the smallest metro economies in the state and account for only a 7% share of GDP. Key Trends

Region and State

From 2010-2016, Arkansas’ GDP grew from $103 billion to $120.7 billion. Using a growth rate index, Arkansas’ GDP grew at a rate of 17.2% [Link to TABLE- Gross Domestic Product by State Growth Index]. Tennessee showed the most sustained economic growth over the 2010-2016 period growing by 30.2%. Although Texas matched Tennessee’s overall growth, the Texas economy was stagnant from 2014-2016. While Arkansas’ growth rate was moderate and sustained, Louisiana’s economy achieved little sustained growth. [Links to TABLE- Gross Domestic Product by State Growth Index and Chart -Index of Growth – GDP by State]

Metropolitan Statistical Area (MSA)

LR-NLR-C, the largest economy in the state, grew from $22.7 billion in 2001 to $39.3 billion in 2015. Again, using a growth rate index, the economy in LR-NLR-C grew at a rate of 77.9% for the 15-year period. [Links to TABLE- Gross Domestic Product by MSA Growth Index and Chart -Index of Growth – GDP by MSA] From 2001-2015, the FSR-metro grew by 138%, almost doubling the economic growth of the LR-NLR-C, Hot Springs, and Jonesboro metros. Fort Smith and Pine Buff grew at lower rates that began to trend negative in 2014. Economic growth in Hot Springs also slowed at the end of the period. The slowing effects of the 2008-2009 recession are evident. While the LR-NLR-C, FSR, and Jonesboro economies expanded during this fifteen- year period, the metro areas of Fort Smith, Hot Springs, and Pine Buff experienced little or no growth.

Methods and Sources

Materials were adapted from the Bureau of Economic Analysis, “Measuring the Economy-A Primer on GDP and National Income Product Accounts,” December 2015.

The Index of Growth measures state GDP growth over the 2010-2016 period using 2010 as the base year. A growth index measures a ratio of a given year’s GDP to a base year’s GDP.

The Index of Growth measures metro GDP growth from 2001-2015 using 2001 as the base year and a ratio of the given year GDP and the base year.
